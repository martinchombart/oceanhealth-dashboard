Salinity tells us where evaporation, rain, ice melt and rivers leave their mark on the ocean. Saltier patches lose more water than they gain (subtropics, the Mediterranean); fresher patches gain it back through rain or melting ice (poles, tropical rain belts).

This pattern is intensifying. As the climate warms, the global water cycle accelerates: salty regions are getting saltier, fresh regions fresher: a clear, measurable signature of climate change.

Salinity also drives the global "ocean conveyor belt." Cold, salty water in the North Atlantic is dense enough to sink to the deep ocean, dragging warm tropical water northward in its wake. This is what gives Western Europe its relatively mild climate. As Greenland melts, the surface freshens and lightens, threatening to slow this circulation with potentially severe consequences for Atlantic weather patterns.
