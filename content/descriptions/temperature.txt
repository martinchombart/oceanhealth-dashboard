Sea temperature is the single clearest fingerprint of how much heat we've added to the planet. The ocean has absorbed roughly 90% of the excess heat from human activity since 1970. Without it, the atmosphere would already be unlivable across much of the world.

The reason is physics. Water absorbs heat far more efficiently than air, so even a small change in ocean temperature represents an immense amount of energy stored, then released slowly over decades. Today's ocean warming is essentially a commitment: even if emissions stopped tomorrow, the heat already stored would keep reshaping our climate for a century.

That stored heat fuels our weather. Each additional degree adds about 7% more water vapor to the atmosphere, the engine of rainfall and storm intensity. Warmer surface waters also drive marine heatwaves, multi-month events that bleach coral reefs and trigger mass die-offs of fish, seabirds and marine mammals.
