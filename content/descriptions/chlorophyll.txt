Chlorophyll-a is the green pigment that lets phytoplankton (microscopic floating plants) perform photosynthesis. Despite being invisible to the naked eye, they produce roughly half of the oxygen on Earth: every second breath you take comes from the ocean, not only from forests on land.

They are also the foundation of every marine food web. Zooplankton eat them, fish eat the zooplankton, and so on up to whales and tuna. When phytoplankton die, they sink to the deep ocean, locking away enormous amounts of carbon: the "biological carbon pump" that has shaped Earth's climate for billions of years.

Climate change is reshaping where they live. Subtropical "ocean deserts" are expanding as warming reduces nutrient mixing. Meanwhile the Arctic is greening: as sea ice retreats, light reaches waters that were once dark, and blooms are pushing northward, a visible but troubling sign of how fast the polar ocean is changing.
