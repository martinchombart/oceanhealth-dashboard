Sea ice is frozen seawater, a thin floating layer that forms each winter and melts each summer, distinct from glaciers and icebergs. Yet that thin layer plays an outsized role in regulating the planet's climate.

The reason is reflectivity. Fresh sea ice reflects 80–90% of incoming sunlight back to space, while dark open ocean absorbs about 94%. As ice melts, the same sunlight that used to bounce off now warms the water, melting more ice; a self-amplifying feedback. This is one reason the Arctic is warming roughly four times faster than the global average.

Ice loss reaches far beyond the poles. As sea ice forms, it expels salt that sinks the surrounding water and powers the global circulation. Less ice means weaker sinking, weaker currents, and disrupted weather patterns, including the jet stream shifts that fuel extreme winters across Europe and North America.
